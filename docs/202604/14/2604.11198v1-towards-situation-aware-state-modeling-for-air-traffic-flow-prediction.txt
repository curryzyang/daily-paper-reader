## Abstract 

Accurate air traffic prediction in the terminal airspace (TA) is pivotal for proactive air traffic management (ATM). However, existing data-driven approaches predominantly rely on time series-based fore-casting paradigms, which inherently overlook critical aircraft state information, such as real-time kinematics and proximity to airspace boundaries. To address this limitation, we propose AeroSense , a di-rect state-to-flow modeling framework for air traffic prediction. Unlike classical time series-based methods that first aggregate air-craft trajectories into macroscopic flow sequences before modeling, AeroSense explicitly represents the real-time airspace situation as adynamic set of aircraft states , enabling the direct processing of a vari-able number of aircraft instead of time series as inputs. Specifically, we introduce a situation-aware state representation that enables AeroSense to sense the instantaneous terminal airspace situation directly from microscopic aircraft states. Furthermore, we design a model architecture that incorporates masked self-attention to capture inter-aircraft interactions, together with two decoupled prediction heads to model heterogeneous flow dynamics across two key functional areas of the TA. Extensive experiments on a large-scale real-world airport dataset demonstrate that AeroSense consistently achieves state-of-the-art performance, validating that direct modeling of microscopic aircraft states yields substantially higher predictive fidelity than time series-based baselines. More-over, the proposed framework exhibits superior robustness during peak traffic periods, achieves Pareto-optimal performance under dayparting multi-object evaluation, and provides meaningful inter-pretability through attention-based visualizations.

## 1 Introduction 

The rapid growth of global air transportation has placed unprece-dented pressure on air traffic management (ATM) systems [ 1 ]. Within this ecosystem, the TA—serving as the interface between en-route airspace and airport surface operations—constitutes a critical bottleneck where congestion, delays, and safety risks frequently concentrate. In particular, the TA comprises two key functional regions, namely the Ap proach Area (AP) and A rea Control Region (AR). The AP corresponds to low-altitude airspace near the ground, which typically exhibits lower traffic volumes, whereas the AR cov-ers higher-altitude airspace with significantly denser traffic flow (illustrated in Figure 1 (a) ). Accurate short-term air traffic flow prediction in both the AP and AR is therefore pivotal for proactive traffic flow management, enabling air traffic controllers to imple-ment sequencing, holding, and spacing strategies ahead of demand surges [2]. With the widespread availability of high-resolution surveillance data such as ADS-B, data-driven approaches have become the dom-inant paradigm for air traffic flow prediction [ 3]. Early studies employed statistical learning models, including support vector machines and gradient boosting trees, to forecast traffic volume from historical observations [ 4]. More recently, deep learning meth-ods—particularly graph neural networks [ 5 – 8] and Transformer-based time series models [ 9]—have achieved strong performance by modeling spatial correlations between airports or temporal de-pendencies in aggregated traffic counts [10]. However, despite these advances, existing approaches largely adhere to a macroscopic modeling paradigm : microscopic aircraft trajectories are first aggregated into macroscopic time series or spatio-temporal flow, after which prediction is performed on these derived time series representations (illustrated in Figure 1 (b)). Such methods rely almost exclusively on statistical correlations learned from aggregated flow data, occasionally augmented with temporal embeddings, while largely ignoring the underlying aircraft-level dynamics. Although computationally convenient, this paradigm

At any given moment 𝑡 , airspace situation of the TA is not enough represented by a time series, but rather a dynamic, unordered set of aircraft , whose cardinality varies continuously due to entry and exit events. Forcing such data into predefined grids or graphs inevitably leads to discretization artifacts, sparsity [ 12 ], and information loss —particularly for fine-grained kinematic cues such as velocity di-rection and boundary proximity. To this end, we propose AeroSense , a novel situation-aware state modeling paradigm that predicts traffic flow directly from microscopic aircraft states. Instead of transforming trajectories into macroscopic time series statistics prior to learning, AeroSense for-mulates the instantaneous airspace situation at time 𝑡 as a dynamic set of aircraft states (illustrated in Figure 1 (c)) and establishes an end-to-end mapping from this set to airspace-level flows at a future horizon 𝑡 + Δ𝑡 . This design preserves the fidelity of individual air-craft kinematics, naturally accommodates variable traffic density, and removes the dependence on look-back window hyperparame-ters, which often have a substantial impact on the generalization of time series-based models. Central to AeroSense is a situation-aware state representation that explicitly encodes boundary proximity, relative kinematic trends, etc., injected into the learning process as inductive biases. Building upon this representation, we design an architecture that combines mechanism of variable-length input handling, masked self-attention for inter-aircraft interaction modeling, and task-decoupled predic-tion heads to capture heterogeneous flow dynamics of AP and AR. Our contributions are summarized as follows: (1) We reformulate air traffic flow prediction from a macro-scopic time-series forecasting problem into a state-to-flow 

learning problem. Instead of relying on aggregated histor-ical flow sequences, we directly model the instantaneous airspace situation as a variable-cardinality set of micro-scopic aircraft states, thereby fundamentally aligning the learning representation with the true physical state of air-craft. (2) We propose AeroSense, a situation-aware modeling frame-work that establishes an end-to-end mapping from aircraft-level states to future regional traffic flows. The framework naturally supports dynamic traffic density, eliminates the need for look-back windows, and avoids information loss caused by spatial or temporal aggregation. (3) We introduce a situation-aware state representation that en-codes boundary interactions, kinematic trends, and control intent as inductive biases. This design enables the model to reason about traffic evolution from underlying flight dynamics rather than purely statistical correlations. (4) Extensive experiments on large-scale real-world terminal airspace data demonstrate that AeroSense consistently out-performs state-of-the-art time-series baselines, particularly under high-density and high-volatility traffic conditions, while providing physically interpretable predictions.

## 3 Experiments 

The experiments aim to answer the following three research ques-tions (RQs): 

• RQ1: How does AeroSense compare with classical time series-based baselines for air traffic flow forecasting? 

• RQ2: How effective are the proposed situation-aware state representation and the designed model architecture? 

• RQ3: How does AeroSense perform under dayparting eval-uation, such as during morning and evening peak periods, beyond overall average performance? 

## 3.1 Experimental Setup 

3.1.1 Downsampling Strategy for Missing-Value Mitigation. A large-scale real-world dataset is collected from the TA of an international airport, spanning from March 1 to October 31, 2025. Aircraft surveil-lance data are sampled at a nominal frequency of 4 seconds. How-ever, ADS-B streams are inherently asynchronous and subject to packet loss, such that directly sampling aircraft states at an instan-taneous timestamp may introduce missing values. To mitigate this issue, we adopt a simple downsampling strategy. Given the nominal sampling interval of 4 seconds, we define a short time-sliced window W = [𝑡 − 𝛿, 𝑡 ] with 𝛿 = 8s, exactly covering two ADS-B stream cycles. For each aircraft, the most recent valid state observed within W is retained to construct the airspace state 

S𝑡 :

S𝑡 = {s𝑖 | timestamp (s𝑖 ) ∈ [ 𝑡 − 𝛿, 𝑡 ], Loc (s𝑖 ) ∈ Ω}. (29) This downsampling procedure yields a missing-value-free snap-shot of the airspace situation at time 𝑡 . After that, the final dataset contains 224,904 samples. To avoid look-ahead issue, the data are split chronologically into training, validation, and test sets with an 8:1:1 ratio, resulting in 179,923 training samples, 22,490 validation samples, and 22,490 test samples. 

3.1.2 Hyperparameter Settings. We summarize the key training settings here; detailed configurations will be released in the open-source code. The latent feature dimension is set to 𝑑 model = 128 ,with 𝐻 = 4 attention heads. Model training is performed using the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of 3 × 10 −4 and a ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler (patience=5). The batch size is set to 64, and training proceeds for up to 100 epochs with early stopping triggered if the validation loss does not improve for 10 consecu-tive epochs. All experiments are implemented in PyTorch 1.13 and conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU (24GB). 

3.1.3 Baselines. To the best of our knowledge, AeroSense is the first model to adopt a state-to-flow modeling paradigm, directly predicting traffic flow from microscopic aircraft states. In contrast, existing state-of-the-art methods primarily operate on aggregated time-series representations and cannot directly process aircraft-level inputs. We therefore benchmark AeroSense against a set of representative state-of-the-art time-series forecasting baselines: 

• Autoformer [ 18 ]: A decomposition-based Transformer that replaces self-attention with auto-correlation to capture series-wise dependencies. 

• Fedformer [ 19 ]: A frequency-enhanced Transformer de-signed to capture global properties through seasonal-trend decomposition in the frequency domain.

Specifically, the day is partitioned into twelve non-overlapping 2-hour intervals, each treated as an independent evaluation objective. As shown in Figure 4, AeroSense consistently achieves low MAE across all time intervals for both the AP and AR areas. In particular, 

AeroSense attains Pareto-optimal performance in the AR region , forming a dominant error frontier that is not surpassed by any baseline under this multi-objective dayparting evaluation. The advantage of AeroSense is especially pronounced during high-volatility periods, such as the morning congestion peak (08:00–10:00). While macroscopic time-series baselines (e.g., Autoformer and FED-former) exhibit substantial performance degradation with sharp error spikes, AeroSense maintains a smooth and stable error profile. This robustness indicates that directly modeling real-time micro-scopic aircraft states effectively mitigates the lag effects inherent in aggregated historical statistics, enabling proactive anticipation of traffic flow transitions rather than reactive forecasting based on past trends.

## 6 Conclusion 

This paper revisits short-term air traffic flow prediction in the TA from a fundamentally different perspective. We argue that the prevailing macroscopic paradigm—based on grid discretization or aggregated time series—introduces an inherent mismatch between the learning representation and the true physical state of air traffic, which is intrinsically a dynamic, variable-cardinality set of aircraft evolving in continuous space. To address this mismatch, we propose AeroSense , the first state-to-flow modeling framework that directly maps microscopic air-craft states to macroscopic traffic flows. By explicitly representing the instantaneous airspace situation as a dynamic set, AeroSense eliminates the dependence on historical look-back windows and avoids information loss caused by spatial or temporal aggregation. Central to the framework is a situation-aware state representation that encodes kinematic trends, boundary proximity, and control intent as inductive biases, enabling the model to reason about traf-fic evolution from flight dynamics. Building upon this represen-tation, AeroSense integrates masked self-attention for modeling inter-aircraft interactions and task-decoupled prediction heads to capture the heterogeneous flow dynamics of different functional regions within the TA. Extensive experiments on a large-scale real-world terminal airspace dataset demonstrate that AeroSense consistently outperforms state-of-the-art time-series baselines. The results confirm that directly modeling microscopic aircraft states yields substantially higher pre-dictive fidelity, particularly under high-density and high-volatility Towards Situation-aware State Modeling for Air Traffic Flow Prediction 

traffic conditions. Beyond average performance gains, AeroSense exhibits strong robustness during peak periods and achieves Pareto-optimal performance under multi-objective dayparting evaluation, highlighting its practical value for proactive air traffic management. Moreover, attention-based visualizations reveal that the model learns task-conditioned and physically interpretable interaction patterns, providing transparent insights into how individual air-craft contribute to regional flow dynamics.
